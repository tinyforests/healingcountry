A movement for the suburbs

There are more gardens than parkland. Start there.

The suburbs are the most cleared, most depleted land in the country — which makes them the place where regeneration matters most. Healing Country is a movement to return indigenous ecology to Australian gardens, record what returns, and connect the gardens into living corridors.

Why the suburbs

Ecology doesn't recognise fences.

Every suburban block once belonged to an ecological community — a specific assemblage of canopy, understorey and groundcover that held soil, water and life together. Most of it is gone. But the land remembers, the records exist, and the plants are still grown nearby.

01
The land is known

Government ecological mapping tells us what grew on almost every address before clearing. The first question is always the same: what once grew here?

02
The gardens exist

Suburbia holds more garden area than parkland. Each block is small; together they are the largest restoration opportunity in the city.

03
The value is real

A recorded, verified ecological garden is measurable value — to councils, to biodiversity programs, and to the steward who tends it.

In development

A garden alone is habitat. Gardens connected are a corridor — and corridors are how ecology moves through a city. Birds don't read title boundaries. Insects don't stop at the fence line.

This movement began with a simple, slightly unreasonable idea: green ecological corridors running through the backyards of Melbourne. The Registry, the finders, the nursery and the reward layer are how we get there — one recorded garden at a time.

On the name

Healing Country borrows a truth long held by First Nations peoples: that Country is living, and that caring for it heals land and people together. This movement works on the unceded lands of the Wurundjeri Woi-wurrung people of the Kulin Nation, whose care for Country spans tens of thousands of years.

We acknowledge Traditional Owners and Elders past and present, and we hold that any healing of this ground begins by listening to those who have always known it.
